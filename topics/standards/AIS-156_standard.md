# AIS 156 — Blog Plan

## Goal
Explain what AIS‑156 requires for traction battery safety in Indian EVs, how it maps to real BMS and pack design decisions, and how engineers demonstrate compliance.

## Audience Angles
- **Engineers / students**: Compliance scope, test categories, documentation flow, link to BMS fault handling and thermal safety.
- **EV enthusiasts**: Why Indian EVs must meet AIS‑156 and what protections it implies for users.

---

## Subtopic Flow

### 1. Hook — Why AIS‑156 Exists
- Recent EV fire incidents drove stricter safety regulation in India.
- AIS‑156 defines how traction batteries must behave under abuse and fault conditions.

### 2. What AIS‑156 Covers (Scope)
- Vehicles covered (2W/3W/4W categories as applicable).
- What is in scope: battery system safety, abuse tests, BMS behavior.
- What is out of scope: emissions, unrelated vehicle systems.

### 3. Battery System Safety Requirements
- Protection against overcharge, over‑discharge, overcurrent, and overtemperature.
- Thermal runaway containment / propagation limits.
- Isolation requirements and HV safety considerations.

### 4. Test Matrix Overview
- Electrical abuse: overcharge, external short, over‑discharge.
- Thermal abuse: high temperature exposure.
- Mechanical abuse: vibration, shock, drop as applicable to pack level.
- Performance verification after tests.

### 5. BMS Compliance Link
- How AIS‑156 requirements translate to BMS features:
  - OV/UV thresholds
  - Fault handling and safe shutdown
  - Thermal monitoring and derating
  - Event logging

### 6. Documentation and Certification Flow
- Type approval process and test reports.
- What manufacturers must document (test setup, pass/fail criteria, traceability).

### 7. Common Engineering Pitfalls
- Poor thermal sensor placement leading to non‑compliance.
- Misconfigured UV/OV thresholds.
- Inadequate insulation monitoring.

### 8. Takeaways
- AIS‑156 is a safety baseline, not a performance standard.
- Compliance is a system property: cell, pack, and BMS must align.
